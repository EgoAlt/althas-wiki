---
title: Faeries
---

**Summary**: An ancestry of uncertain, likely recent origin, bound almost entirely to church and convent life. Trusted across Althas as message carriers precisely because of how little they care about anyone's business but their flock's.

No scholar has a solid account of where faeries come from. There are no records of them in older scriptures and no faerie bones in the ruins of ancient settlements, which suggests their origin is fairly recent as these things go. The best-supported theory, thin as it is, comes from the degenerative nickname "failed angel": faeries have round, childlike faces, small frames, and wings, all reminiscent of the angels of holy scripture, and most flocks live in convents or near churches, working for the local clergy. Whether they're a botched attempt to summon angels into the world is unproven, but it's the leading guess for lack of anything better.

Faeries are peaceful and remarkably uninterested in the world outside their own flock. Even sociable ones manage nothing warmer than a cold, distant friendship with a local church member; most keep to other faeries entirely. This incuriosity is likely exactly why they became Althas's message carriers: no one else could be trusted not to read a private letter out of curiosity, but a faerie handed an open letter would rather not bother. The only writing they take any real interest in is holy scripture and the (typically dull) writings of other faeries about flock life and church trivia.

Faeries not bound to a cleric are extremely rare. When it happens, it's usually trauma or survival driving it: being chosen as a priest's concubine (a fate some churches encourage, to keep faeries as a distraction from the nobility's own children), a master who exaggerates punishment for late deliveries, or worse.

[[rosestripe|Rosestripe]], of the [[convent-of-saint-trefan|Convent of Saint Trefan]], is an exception to the incuriosity: she taught herself to read so she could study scripture on her own, and picks up outside books out of genuine curiosity, a trait the monks and her fellow faeries consider eccentric but harmless.
